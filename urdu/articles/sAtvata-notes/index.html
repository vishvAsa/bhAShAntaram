Motivation

Urdu is an ersatz Islamicate ideolect that largely emerged out of conscious rejection of broader Indian culture.
Urdu-e-Mualla = Shahjahanabad. It was primarily the literary language of Muslims though.

Persianization

dates

Persianization dates to the late 17th to early 18th century at the latest:

شاہ صاحب فرموده که شما زبان دکنی را گذاشته ریخته را موافق اردوئے معلی شاهجهان آباد موزون بکنید که تا موجب شهرت و رواج و مقبول خاطر طبعان عالی مزاج گردد۔

Šāh Saʿdullāh Gulšan (to Walī Dakkanī):

“You should set aside the Dakkanī language and shape this Rēk͟htah in accordance with the Urdū-i Muʿallā-i Šāhjahānābād, so that it may become a cause of fame, circulation, and acceptance among people of refined taste.

Also:

می گویند که در شاه جهان آباد دهلی نیز آمدہ بود۔ بخدمت میان گلشن صاحب رفت و از اشعار خود پاره خواند۔ میان صاحب فرمود که این هم مضامین فارسی که بیکار افتاده اندر در ریخته خود بکار ببرد از تو که محاسبه خواهد گرفت۔

It is said that he (i.e. Walī Dakkanī) also came to Šāhjahānābād (in Dihlī) during the reign of Šāh Jahān. He (Walī) went to attend upon Miyāñ Gulšan Ṣāḥib and recited a portion of his own poetry. Miyāñ Ṣāḥib (Šāh Saʿdullāh Gulšan) remarked:

“These are Persian themes that have fallen into disuse and lie neglected. Use them yourself; from you, who will demand an accounting?”

Pervasiveness

It’s also not true that authors in Delhi used a far less Persianizing language.

Ġālib is actually a great example, as his poetry was often thoroughly inundated with Persian and Arabic loans, for example: Hai wasl hijr-i ʿālam-i tamkīn o zabt meñ maʿśūq-i śokh o ʿāśiq-i dīwānah cāhiye

Themes

“Early Urdu poets never so much as mention the great physical features of India-its Himalayas, its rivers like the Ganges, the Jumna, the Indus, the Narmada, the Godavari, etc., but obscure mountains and streams of Persia, and the rivers of Central Asia are always there Indian flowers, Indian plants are unknown, only Persian flowers and plants, which the poet could see only in a garden.
There was a deliberate shutting of the eye to everything Indian, every thing not mentioned or treated in Persian poetry.
The first Urdu poets, deeply moved by the manifest decay of Muslim political power in the 18th century, sought to escape from a world they did not like by taking refuge in the garden seclusion of Persian poetry, the atmosphere of which they imported into Urdu.”
- Suniti Kumar Chatterji

Suffix Substitution

Moreover, the replacement extends further than isolated lexemes.

The Persian izāfat construct is productive in Urdu while it is limited to stock phrases (e.g. mādar-i watan, rang-i mahfil, etc.) in Hindi.

It is often employed in poetry in lieu of postpositions like kā/kī/ke.

We also see loans of entire Arabic prepositional phrases into Urdu.

This includes loans such as filhāl (< fī l-ħāl), bilkul (< bi l-kull), and alahdā (< alā ħidah) which are common to both Urdu and Hindi but also less common loans that are used mainly in Urdu such as fī l-haqīqat, fī l-wāqiʿ, fī l-badīh (e.g. “sak͟ht muškil hai fī l-badīh kahnā…”), bi l-k͟husūs, bi l-ʿumūm, bi l-āk͟hir, bi l-ittifāq, bi l-wazāħat, bi jinsi-hi (note also the use of Arabic 3rd person enclitic pronoun in majrūr state), ʿalā l-ʿumūm (e.g. “ġārat ʿalā l-ʿumūm thī, thā hukm-i qatl-i ʿām…”), ʿalā l-ittisāl, etc.

Moreover, Persian past participles are typically loaned directly into Urdu as standalone adjectives rather than appending past participle modifiers like [kiyā] hūā, etc. to the verb.

For example:

Madīne kā safar hai aur maĩ nam-dīdah, nam-dīdah
jabī̃ afsurdah, afsurdah;
qadam laġzīdah, laġzīdah čalā hū̃ ek mujrim kī tarah maĩ jānib-e-Tayyibah
nazar šarmindah, šarmindah; badan larzīdah, larzīdah
kisī ke hāth ne mujh ko sahārā de diyā, varna
kahā̃ maĩ aur kahā̃ ye rāste pečīdah, pečīdah
kahā̃ maĩ aur kahā̃ us raużah-e-aqdas kā nazzāra
nazar us samt uṭhtī hai magar duzdīdah, duzdīdah ġulāmān-e-Muḥammad dūr se pahčāne jāte haĩ dil-i girvīdah, girvīdah; sar-i šorīdah, šorīdah Madīne jā ke ham samjhe taqaddus kis ko kahte haĩ havā pākīzah, pākīzah; fazā sanjīdah, sanjīdah basārat kho gaʾī lekin basīrat to salāmat hai Madīna ham ne dekhā hai magar nādīdah, nādīdah vahī Iqbāl jis ko nāz thā kal k͟huš-mizājī par firāq-e-Tayyibah mẽ rahtā hai ab rañjīdah, rañjīdah

Here we see the past participles namdīdah, afsurdah, laġzīdah, šarmindah, larzīdah, pečīdah, duzdīdah, girvīdah, šorīdah, sanjīdah, nādīdah, and rañjīdah.

While Sanskritized Hindi does borrow lexemes from Sanskrit and occasionally even borrows words with endings attached (kṛpayā, sambhavataḥ, etc.), the structural loaning from Persian/Arabic in Urdu is deeper than Sanskrit structural borrowing in High Hindi.

There is no parallel to “bi-jinsi-hi” in Sanskritized Hindi.

Moreover, compounding through samāsas is structurally native.

It is seen throughout Sanskrit, Prakrit, and Apabhraṃśa literature and cannot be equated to the productive izāfat-construct (i.e. foreign phrasal syntax) in Urdu.
